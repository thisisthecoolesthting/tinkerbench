8. Through-hole vs surface-mount soldering for beginners

By Eli Frost · Senior Editor

Published May 12, 2026 · Last reviewed May 12, 2026

Through-hole vs Surface-Mount Soldering for Beginners: What’s Right for You?

If you’re new to electronics assembly or repair, one of the first challenges you’ll face is choosing between through-hole and surface-mount soldering techniques. Each method has its own learning curve, tooling requirements, and practical applications. Understanding these differences early can save you frustration, protect your components, and get your projects up and running faster.

What Is Through-Hole Soldering?

Through-hole soldering involves placing electronic components with wire leads into pre-drilled holes on a printed circuit board (PCB). The leads are then soldered on the board’s opposite side, creating a strong mechanical and electrical connection.

This method has been the standard for decades and is often used in kits, hobby projects, and devices requiring rugged connections. Components like resistors, capacitors, and connectors with thick leads are typical candidates for through-hole mounting.

What Is Surface-Mount Soldering?

Surface-mount technology (SMT) mounts components directly onto the surface of the PCB without leads passing through holes. These components are significantly smaller — often just a few millimeters in size — and are soldered to pads on the board, usually using solder paste and precise heat application methods like reflow ovens or hot air stations.

SMT is dominant in modern electronics manufacturing due to its space-saving advantages and suitability for automated assembly, but it can be trickier for beginners.

When to Use Through-Hole Soldering

Through-hole soldering is particularly well-suited for:

  • Beginners and hobbyists: The larger component size and visible leads make placement easier.
  • Kits and prototyping: Many kits and hobby PCBs are designed for through-hole components because they make assembly straightforward and reduce frustration.
  • Mechanical stress areas: Components that might suffer bending, unplugging, or connector plugs benefit from stronger solder joints.
  • Repairs and modifications: Components can easily be desoldered and replaced without extensive heat exposure or risk of damaging adjacent parts.

For example, if you’re assembling a DIY amplifier kit or a microcontroller breakout board designed for education, through-hole is often the path of least resistance.

When to Use Surface-Mount Soldering

Surface-mount soldering becomes advantageous when:

  • Space is a premium: SMT parts are tiny, allowing more connections and features on smaller PCBs.
  • Automated production: If you plan to make multiple units or more advanced PCBs, SMT is industry standard.
  • You want to develop precision soldering skills: While challenging for beginners, SMT helps develop hand-eye coordination and familiarizes you with processes used in commercial manufacturing.
  • Working with modern electronics: Most modern devices use SMT, so if repairing smartphones, laptops, or compact gadgets, learning SMT is essential.

Beginners might try SMT slow and steady — practice on simple, larger-LEAD devices or with practice kits designed specifically for learning surface-mount soldering.

Common Pitfalls for Beginners

No matter the method, some common traps include:

  • Overheating components: Excessive heat damages parts; use the appropriate temperature soldering iron and short contact time.
  • Applying too much solder: This can cause shorts, especially in SMT where pads are closer together.
  • Poor soldering technique: A cold joint (dull, cracked, or incomplete solder) leads to unreliable connections.
  • Improper tool selection: Using a dull tip or inappropriate heat source will frustrate beginners and damage PCBs.
  • Inadequate workspace and lighting: Working under poor light or without magnification makes tiny SMT parts frustrating.

Tips for Success with Both Methods

  1. Invest in good tools:

    • For through-hole, a decent temperature-controlled soldering iron with conical or chisel tips works best.
    • For SMT, fine conical or needle tips plus quality tweezers and maybe a hot air station or reflow oven will make all the difference.

  2. Use quality solder:

    • 60/40 or 63/37 tin-lead solder (for hobby use only, respecting local laws) is easier to work with than lead-free, which requires higher heat.
    • Thin solder wire (0.5mm or less) helps control solder quantity, especially for SMT.

  3. Practice good technique:

    • Heat the joint (both pad and lead for through-hole) before applying solder to get good adhesion.
    • For SMT, be patient and use just enough solder paste or wire. Reflow evenly.

  4. Start with kits and practice boards:

    • Many beginner kits use through-hole parts.
    • SMT practice kits allow you to build up confidence incrementally.

  5. Work in a well-lit, ventilated area:

    • Use magnification tools (LED lamps with magnifiers or head-mounted lenses).

Final Thoughts

For most beginners, starting with through-hole soldering makes sense because the components are larger, the work area is less cramped, and mistakes are easier to fix. It’s the foundation for solid soldering skills. Once comfortable, you can progressively move on to surface-mount soldering, which will open up more possibilities, especially for compact and modern designs.

Remember, patience and practice are key. Don’t rush into SMT until you’ve nailed through-hole basics unless you specifically need the skill for repairing or building modern devices.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can I solder surface-mount components with a regular soldering iron?
A: Yes, with patience and a fine-tip soldering iron, you can solder larger SMT parts manually. For very small components, specialized tools like hot air stations or reflow ovens make the job easier and more reliable.

Q: Does through-hole soldering produce stronger joints than surface-mount?
A: Generally, yes. The leads passing through the PCB create a mechanical connection that withstands physical stress better than SMT, which relies solely on solder pads.

Q: Which solder should beginners use for best results?
A: For hobbyists, rosin-core solder with a 60/40 or 63/37 tin-lead ratio is easier to work with because it melts at lower temperatures and flows more smoothly. Lead-free solder requires higher heat and can be less forgiving for new users.
