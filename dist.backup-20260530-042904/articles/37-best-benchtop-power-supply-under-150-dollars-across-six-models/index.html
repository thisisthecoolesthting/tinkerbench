37. Best benchtop power supply under 150 dollars across six models

By Eli Frost · Senior Editor

Published May 12, 2026 · Last reviewed May 12, 2026

If you’re a hobbyist electronics maker or a bench technician on a budget, finding a reliable benchtop power supply under $150 can feel like a balancing act between features, precision, and build quality. You want something accurate enough for delicate circuit work, with stable output and decent current capabilities, but without emptying your wallet. This article reviews six solid benchtop power supplies priced under $150, focusing on what really matters: precision, durability, ease of use, and overall value for makers and kit builders.

Criteria for Choosing a Benchtop Power Supply Under $150

Before diving into the models, let’s clarify the key performance and feature criteria for budget-friendly bench supplies:

  • Voltage and current range: 0–30V and up to around 3A is typical and sufficient for most hobby projects.
  • Voltage and current resolution: Fine voltage steps (~10mV) and current limiting with accurate readout matter for precision.
  • Stability and ripple: Low ripple (ideally below 50mV peak-to-peak) is important to avoid interference in sensitive circuits.
  • Display type: Clear, readable digital displays (LED or LCD) allow precise control and quick status checks.
  • Protection features: Overcurrent, short circuit, and thermal protection extend equipment life and safeguard your projects.
  • User interface: Knobs and buttons that feel smooth and precise are key for setting output without frustration.
  • Size and build quality: Compact footprints save bench space; a metal chassis tends to last longer.
  • Extras: USB ports or multiple outputs are nice but rarely critical at this price point.

Detailed Reviews and Usage Scenarios

1. Linear-style Digital Supply (Rank 1)

If precision and reliability are your priorities, linear power supplies stand out. While heavier and slightly less energy efficient, their low ripple and noise are critical when powering RF or sensitive analog projects. The digital controls and dual LED display mean you can dial-in voltage and current limit quickly and confidently. The metal chassis improves heat dissipation and durability. Ideal for advanced hobbyists who frequently test sensors, microcontrollers, and audio circuits.

2. Switch-mode with LCD (Rank 2)

Offers a versatile balance of form and function. The switch-mode design cuts weight significantly and boosts efficiency but usually at the cost of some ripple and noise. An LCD screen with menu-based controls allows setting multiple output modes and saving configurations. Good choice if weight and size matter, and you’re working mostly on digital circuits or charging portable kits. Be aware that ripple can sometimes introduce subtle interference in RF testing.

3. Linear-style Analog Control (Rank 3)

Analog linear supplies with tactile knobs give an immediate and intuitive feel. The very low output noise makes it prime for test benches where clean power is essential. However, lacking modern safety features means more care is needed to avoid accidental shorts and damage. This classic design works well as a reliable backup supply or educational tool for beginners learning fundamental electronics safety and measurement.

4. Switch-mode Digital 0–5A (Rank 4)

For projects demanding more current like LED driver testing, motors, or power amplifier prototypes, a 5A-rated switch mode supply extends capability without breaking the bank. The tradeoff includes somewhat more ripple and less precise control at low output power, but the extra current range expands your experimental possibilities significantly.

5. Compact USB Meter Combo (Rank 5)

Ultra-portable and feature-packed for the price, these combine power supply outputs with USB charging ports and digital meters. They fit nicely in cramped workspaces or mobile setups. Current max is usually lower, and ripple can be higher, so their best use is quick prototyping, handheld device powering, or low-current sensor evaluation.

6. DIY Kit Power Supply (Rank 6)

If you want the lowest price and a hands-on learning experience, an electronics kit supply is a viable starter. You assemble basic components onto a chassis and test your build. Accuracy and protections tend to be lower, making this ideal only once you understand bench supply basics.

Practical Considerations for Beginners

  • Precision vs Price: Look for at least 10mV resolution and low ripple; these features matter more than flashy interfaces.
  • Protection features: Overcurrent and thermal protections prevent costly mistakes with fragile components and your supply.
  • Form factor: A heavy metal chassis is tough and reduces interference; for portability, a lighter switch-mode unit might suit better.
  • User control: Rotate knobs usually offer finer control than buttons or menus on cheap supplies.
  • Brand-name vs compatible accessories: Invest in good quality test leads and banana plugs; cheap third-party accessories can increase resistance and reduce stability readings.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can I power a microcontroller board safely with these supplies?
A: Yes, any of these supplies with adjustable voltage and current limiting are suitable for microcontrollers. Just make sure to set the voltage and current limits before connecting sensitive components.

Q: How important is voltage ripple for my hobby projects?
A: Ripple is the fluctuation of the DC output voltage. High ripple can cause erratic behavior in analog and RF circuits. If you mainly work on digital electronics or LED projects, a bit more ripple is less critical.

Q: Can I use third-party or compatible cartridges and accessories with these supplies?
A: While “cartridges” are not common in power supplies, compatible accessories like test leads can vary in quality. Opt for well-made cables and connectors to maintain accuracy and avoid interference.

Choosing a benchtop power supply under $150 requires weighing accuracy, current range, and durability against your practical needs. Hopefully, this review helps you find a workhorse power supply that fits your bench and budget without gimmicks or compromises that matter less than you think.
