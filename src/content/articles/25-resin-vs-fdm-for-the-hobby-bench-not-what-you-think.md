## Resin vs FDM for the Hobby Bench: Not What You Think

When it comes to 3D printing around the hobby bench, the conversation often boils down to two main contenders: resin and FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling). Each technology has its fans and detractors, but the truth is far more nuanced than the usual “resin = detail, FDM = cheap and easy” narrative. If you’re trying to decide which type of 3D printing suits your projects, you’ll want to consider a broader range of factors that affect usability, cost, safety, and the kind of results you really need.

Let’s unpack some of the common assumptions, and explore what choosing resin or FDM printing really means for your hobby bench setup.

---

## The Usual Story: Resin for Detail, FDM for Basics

Most hobbyists are told resin printers excel at fine detail—think miniatures, intricate parts, jewelry prototypes. The laser or LCD curing process creates high-resolution layers often well below 100 microns, revealing details that FDM printers, which lay down melted filament in thicker layers, typically can’t match.

On the flip side, FDM printers are often pitched as budget-friendly “workhorses” that handle larger, durable parts well, but with visible layer lines and less refinement in details.

But these points are just the surface. Let's dive deeper.

---

## Print Surface Finish: More Than Just Layer Height

It’s true that resin prints offer a smooth, nearly polished finish thanks to their layer-by-layer curing of liquid resin. However, these flawless surfaces come at a price—post-processing. Resin prints usually require:

- Washing in isopropyl alcohol or specialized cleaning fluids  
- Careful curing under UV light to fully harden  
- Removal of supports that can be fiddly and prone to marring the print  
- Handling resin safely to avoid skin irritation or allergic reactions  

Many hobbyists underestimate how much time and effort these steps add.

Meanwhile, FDM prints come off the machine largely ready to use, especially if your needs are functional parts or prototypes. Sure, visible layer lines exist, but sanding, filing, or acetone vapor smoothing (for some filament types) can improve the finish without toxic post-processing.

---

## Material Properties: Resin is Not Always Stronger

A common misconception is that resin prints are inherently stronger than FDM prints. In reality, resin's cured plastic tends to be more brittle and can chip or break under stress. Some specialized resins improve toughness, but standard hobby resins often produce parts better suited for display than functional use.

FDM prints use thermoplastic filaments like PLA, ABS, PETG, or Nylon, which generally offer greater impact resistance and flexibility. Layer adhesion and infill settings also let you fine-tune strength. For tools, jigs, or parts subjected to mechanical stress on the bench, FDM may be the smarter choice.

---

## Cost and Consumables: Beyond the Printer Price Tag

While resin printers have become more affordable in recent years, the ongoing cost of resin, cleaning fluids, and replacement vats or FEP films can add up quickly. Resins can run significantly more expensive per liter than filament, and inventory management (storing unused resin securely, monitoring shelf life) adds complexity.

FDM filament spools tend to be cheaper, longer-lasting, and easier to store. Plus, you can select from various specialty filaments for flexibility, heat resistance, or other properties without dramatically increasing cost.

From an economic perspective, FDM often offers a lower barrier for hobbyists planning frequent printing.

---

## Health and Safety: A Crucial Consideration Often Overlooked

Resin printing involves handling uncured resins, which are irritants and possible sensitizers. Proper gloves, ventilation, and careful cleaning routines are essential to avoid skin exposure and inhalation of fumes.

FDM printing isn’t without risks—especially with filaments like ABS that emit potentially harmful fumes—but the concerns are generally milder, and print areas can be more easily ventilated. Filament handling doesn’t require as much protective gear.

For busy hobbyists or those sharing space with family, health and safety practicalities may strongly influence printer choice.

---

## Print Volume and Speed: Resins Can Be Slow and Small

Resin prints are typically limited in build volume compared to many FDM models. While high-detail small parts are a resin strength, larger models can become time-prohibitive or necessitate splitting into multiple prints.

Layer curing times combined with peeling cycles make resin prints slower per unit height. FDM printers, though slower in finer layers, often excel at producing larger or many parts in a single run faster.

If your hobby bench projects involve bigger items like model parts, drone frames, or custom tools, an FDM printer often scales better.

---

## Practical Tips for Choosing Between Resin and FDM

If you’re still unsure, here are some practical questions to guide your decision:

- **What kind of projects are you printing mostly?**  
  Highly detailed miniatures and jewelry detail? Lean resin. Functional prototypes, tools, or large-scale models? Lean FDM.

- **How much post-processing time can you dedicate?**  
  If you want parts that are more "plug and play," FDM saves time.

- **How critical is strength and durability?**  
  FDM parts typically better for mechanical use.

- **What about your workspace setup?**  
  Resin printing needs good ventilation, careful handling, and space for post-processing.

- **Cost sensitivity?**  
  Consider ongoing costs of consumables.

- **Your comfort level with chemicals and cleaning?**  
  If glove-and-ventilation protocols feel cumbersome, FDM might fit you better.

---

## Can You Combine Both? Absolutely

Many seasoned hobbyists use both technologies side-by-side. Use resin printers for detailed parts you want to look great on display, and FDM printers for larger, functional parts where finish isn’t as critical.

This hybrid approach lets you leverage strengths from both technologies without over-committing or compromising.

---

## Final Thoughts: The Right Tool for Your Bench is the One That Fits Your Workflow

Resin and FDM each bring unique strengths, but neither is “better” across the board. The key is understanding how their differences align with your particular hobby needs, tolerance for post-processing, workspace conditions, and budget.

Next time you hear “resin is only for miniatures” or “FDM is just for hobbyist beginners,” remember there’s much more to the story. Consider your workflow holistically, and you might find the best 3D printing setup for your bench is not what you originally thought.
