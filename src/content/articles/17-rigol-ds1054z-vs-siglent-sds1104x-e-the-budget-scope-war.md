## 17. Rigol DS1054Z vs Siglent SDS1104X-E: The Budget Scope War

When it comes to budget oscilloscopes, two models consistently stand out: the Rigol DS1054Z and the Siglent SDS1104X-E. Both have earned solid reputations among hobbyists, educators, and professionals starting out in electronics thanks to their feature sets and affordability. But which offers the better value for your money in 2024?

In this article, we’ll break down the strengths, weaknesses, and key differences between these two popular oscilloscopes to help you make an informed choice.

---

## Overview of the Contenders

### Rigol DS1054Z

- **Bandwidth:** 50 MHz (official), upgradeable to 100 MHz
- **Channels:** 4 analog
- **Sample Rate:** 1 GSa/s (single channel)
- **Memory Depth:** 12 Mpts
- **Price Range:** Typically lower-end budget bracket

The Rigol DS1054Z started the trend of affordable 4-channel digital oscilloscopes with surprisingly generous specs. It’s lauded for its solid build, user-friendly interface, and a capable feature set that eclipsed many scopes twice its price at launch. The hidden bandwidth upgrade (to 100 MHz) further sweetened the deal for many users.

### Siglent SDS1104X-E

- **Bandwidth:** 100 MHz (official)
- **Channels:** 4 analog
- **Sample Rate:** 1 GSa/s (single channel)
- **Memory Depth:** 14 Mpts
- **Price Range:** Slightly higher than Rigol DS1054Z

The Siglent SDS1104X-E arrived as a direct competitor, often considered an evolution with some refinement. It features higher official bandwidth, comparable sample rate, and deeper memory. It has also gained points for improved display quality and additional analysis features, positioning itself as a strong contender for those willing to spend a bit more.

---

## Bandwidth and Performance

Bandwidth dictates the maximum frequency an oscilloscope can accurately measure. Generally, a scope’s bandwidth should be at least five times the highest signal frequency you expect to analyze.

- **Rigol DS1054Z:** The official bandwidth is 50 MHz, which is adequate for many hobbyist and educational projects. However, it has an unofficial upgrade method that unlocks 100 MHz bandwidth via a firmware tweak, though this may void warranty or carry risks.

- **Siglent SDS1104X-E:** Comes standard with 100 MHz bandwidth and no need for hacks. This is comfortable for 100 MHz and below signals without worrying about performance limitations.

In practice, users note that while the Rigol can perform nicely at 50 MHz and with the hidden upgrade effectively at 100 MHz, the Siglent’s 100 MHz bandwidth is immediately accessible and officially supported.

---

## Channels and Sample Rate

Both scopes offer 4 analog input channels and sample rates of up to 1 GSa/s on a single channel.

- The multi-channel sampling rate is effectively halved when using more than one channel, which is common across many oscilloscopes at this price range.
- Both scopes handle this similarly, enabling the user to comfortably analyze signals on multiple channels without significant degradation for common frequencies.

---

## Memory Depth and Capture Quality

Memory depth impacts how long and detailed a waveform the scope can capture at high resolution.

- **Rigol DS1054Z:** Provides 12 Mpts of memory, which allows for reasonably long waveform capture and detailed analysis.
- **Siglent SDS1104X-E:** Offers slightly more — 14 Mpts memory depth — giving finer granularity and longer capture windows.

While this difference is not massive, the Siglent does extend the buffer size advantageously for burst or complex capture scenarios.

---

## Display and User Interface

- **Rigol DS1054Z:** Features a clear, responsive 7-inch LCD with straightforward navigation. Menus and knob functions are intuitive, with solid documentation and community support.
- **Siglent SDS1104X-E:** Also sports a 7-inch color display, but often praised for better brightness and contrast. The interface is polished and allows for quick access to advanced features like math functions, FFT, and serial decoding.

For users who rely heavily on a more modern display and advanced scope functionalities out of the box, the Siglent edges ahead.

---

## Built-in Features and Analysis

Both scopes come loaded with a wide range of analysis tools tailored for electronics work:

- **Math functions:** Addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, FFT, and others.
- **Serial decoding:** Support for I2C, SPI, UART, CAN, LIN protocols.
- **Persistence mode:** For visualizing analog signal stability over time.
- **Triggering:** Edge, pulse width, video, slope, and other advanced trigger types.

The Siglent often receives praise for the slightly more extensive built-in protocol decoders and more refined triggering options. Rigol’s firmware updates have improved its capabilities over time, but Siglent remains competitive and sometimes ahead in this area.

---

## Software and PC Connectivity

Both models provide PC connectivity options, allowing users to capture and analyze waveforms on a computer.

- **Rigol:** Software connectivity is good with remote control and waveform streaming, supported on Windows and some Linux builds.
- **Siglent:** Also offers solid PC software, including enhanced cloud sharing features and more frequent updates.

Neither scope is a perfect replacement for dedicated lab PC software, but both do well to integrate into modern workflows.

---

## Build Quality and Reliability

- **Rigol DS1054Z:** Known for a robust metal chassis, solid knobs and buttons, and durability under daily use. However, some units may occasionally suffer from minor backlight or fan noise issues.
- **Siglent SDS1104X-E:** Also exhibits solid build quality with slightly quieter operation and improved cooling design in newer builds.

Both are durable enough for bench work and education, but some users find the Siglent a bit quieter and cooler under heavy use.

---

## Price and Value

- **Rigol DS1054Z:** Usually found at a lower price point, it offers tremendous value for users on a tight budget, especially if willing to explore bandwidth upgrades.
- **Siglent SDS1104X-E:** Slightly more expensive, yet justifies cost with official higher bandwidth, deeper memory, and a better display and feature set.

Choosing between them often comes down to budget flexibility versus official specs and ease of use.

---

## Who Should Buy Which?

### Choose the **Rigol DS1054Z** if:

- You need a reliable 4-channel scope with great community support.
- Your projects don’t exceed 50 MHz or you’re comfortable applying a bandwidth upgrade.
- Your budget is tighter, and every dollar counts.
- You value proven hardware with long track record in hobbyist circles.

### Choose the **Siglent SDS1104X-E** if:

- You want official 100 MHz bandwidth without tweaks.
- You desire a slightly better screen and preset advanced features.
- You prefer a scope with marginally better memory depth.
- You have a bit more budget and want a scope to grow with your experience.

---

## Conclusion: The Budget Scope War Verdict

The Rigol DS1054Z and Siglent SDS1104X-E are both excellent oscilloscopes in the affordable 4-channel category and represent some of the best value for electronics enthusiasts and early professionals today.

If you’re budget-conscious and okay with some DIY, the Rigol DS1054Z offers tremendous bang for your buck and a heavyweight foundation in budget scopes. But if you want a modern feature set, official bandwidth, and a refined interface with better display, the Siglent SDS1104X-E is worth the modest price premium.

Ultimately, both models are capable workhorses whose strengths cater to slightly different needs and preferences. Whichever you pick, you’ll be well-equipped for most electronics debugging, design, and learning tasks without breaking the bank.
